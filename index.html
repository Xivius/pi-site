How Many Digits of Pi Do Know?

What is π?

Pi started out as a letter in the Greek alphabet. Its uppercase form is П, which you may or may not recognize. It’s more likely you’ve seen its lower case form π. It’s also likely that you’ve seen π being used in a mathematical context, which is the main focus of this webpage. In mathematics, π is an irrational mathematical constant, which means that it contains an infinite sequence of non-repeating decimal digits to the right of the decimal point. Ancient civilizations around the world discovered its existence over 4000 years ago and began calculating it. However, Archimedes of Syracuse was the first mathematician to create an algorithm for calculating π; therefore, it can also be called “Archimedes’ constant”. The fact that Archimedes was Greek may explain how the letter ‘π’ originally came to be chosen as the representation of the value 3.141592653589... In the following centuries, we credit the famous mathematician Euler for popularizing the modern use of the ‘π’ symbol to express this value as well as its increased usage in mathematics.

Fun Fact:

If you are more heavily involved with mathematics, you’ve also seen П used as well as π. П is used to express the product of a sequence. For example,
П k=1 5 k = 1 × 2 × 3 × 4 × 5

Why π?

π is a significant value that appears in nature quite often. As a result, it appears in the natural sciences and technology related fields. π has become so well known because of this that it’s used periodically in pop culture. March 14th (3/14) is named Pi Day in dedication to all the times π has contributed to science and technology. In the year 2015, Pi Day reached a once in a century milestone: 3/14/15 (3.1415 vs the typical 3.14). Memorizing the endless digits of π has even turned into a community competition. People also compete for the world record with current world record being 70,030 digits (see source below)!

How is π Used?

π most commonly used for calculations involving circular regions of space. Common examples include area of a circle, volume of a sphere, and trignometry (especially for the unit circle). It has also appeared in a number areas with no direct connection to circles such as Einstein’s field equation of general relativity. See this section for more on How To Use π.

Who Encounters π?

Prior to the 20th century, π was primarily of interest to mathematicians, who invented many different algorithms in attempt to generate a more precise value of π. In the late-20th, it became the interest of computer scientists to use computers to put these algorithms to the test. In fact, this is one of the metrics that humanity uses to demonstrate advances in computing technology. Around this time, space travel and other mission critical systems required a certain precision of π for their calculations. NASA acknowledges its use of π today in this article. Many areas of STEM (Science Technology Engineering Mathematics) outside mathematics require scientists and engineers to utilize areas of math that deal with π, such as trignometry, Fourier Transforms, and topology. As a result, depending on which scientists and engineers you talk to, they could be encountering π on a consistent basis, especially those who are in research.
